THE HELPFULNESS OF COPYRIGHT MISUSE IN DMCA CIRCUMVENTION

The article written in 2014 by Lauren Stewart, explores the freedom that DMCA has granted copyright holders to implement anticircumvention protection on their software beyond what it was originally intended. It uses the MDY v. Blizzard case as a case study for this kind of abuse. Moreover copyright holders are hindering new legislation about DMCA by lobbying against it.

Aiden

Piracy in the Digital Age - University of California, San Diego

The peer-reviewed article titled ‘Piracy in the Digital Age’ was written by the following five authors: Dana Dashstrom, Nathan Farrington, Daniel Gobera, Ryan Roemer, and Nabil Schear. IT was published by the University of California in San Diego in 2006. This article focusses on the digital side of piracy. The article beings by introducing piracy by explaining the technical background and motivation and consequences for pirating. It continues by talking about the history behind piracy. It begins by talking about the history when piracy first come about then continues by talking about piracy in the 20th century then concludes by talking about recent history. The author continues by talking more in depth about software piracy and its origins. Further, they then discuss the technical parts behind software piracy including, open and close source programs as well as software locks. Following that, they discuss the legalities behind this piracy and provides some legal battles that have occurred due to these laws. The last topic they cover is the Digital Millennium Copyright Act and stopping the lock pickers. Finally, concludes the article by summarising the main points. The authors leave the reader thinking about how fast digital piracy is accelerating and that more problems are going to arise due to this.

Can the madness be monetized? An exploratory survey of music piracy and acquisition behaviour

Written by Storm Gloor and Clyde Philip Rolston, this 2010 article looks at digital piracy in the music industry and more specifically, why people pirate music and how it effects the industry. This is a good article as it presents some interesting ideas regarding the philosophy behind piracy, focusing more on why people do it, and suggests possible solutions/outcomes based on adapting to how people think instead of punishing them. The findings are based off a survey that asks “why non-payers acquired tracks without paying”, “Tracks downloaded per month”, and suggested pricing for music, among other things. The survey results are included in the article and provide information from several demographics. The article was part of the annual peer reviewed MEIEA (music and entertainment industry educators association) journal, which is made up of educators and academics in the music and entertainment industry.

Music Piracy and the Audio Home Recording Act

The peer reviewed article by Tia Hall discusses the effect of piracy on the music industry and the approaches companies have taken against the acts of piracy which are harming the industry. Tia showcases the facts of in which some “Major Music Labels develop Anti-Piracy Technology” with examples of Sony’s developed anti-piracy technology used against Piracy. Showing how the music industry is affected and how society responds to it through anti-piracy measures being taken by digital content owners and the United States legislature to combat piracy and evaluate them in light of the AHRA.

Software Piracy in Research: A Moral Analysis

Software Piracy in Research: A Moral Analysis was an article written by Gary Santillanes and Ryan Marshall Felder and published in August 2015 to ‘Science and Engineering Ethics’ issue 21.4 page 967-977, a journal discussing ethical issues faced by modern scientists and engineers. This article discusses the need that modern researchers have for advanced software to conduct concise and effective research and addresses the fact that not all researchers can afford the software they need. Many researchers who believe their work enhance the quality of human life are often found in an ethical dilemma deciding if they should break the law and pirate software that they could otherwise not afford or simply restrict their research to what can be completed with the free software that is available. This article argues that many scientists moral code drives them to seriously consider the illegal piracy of such software in order continue their pursuit in valuable research.
